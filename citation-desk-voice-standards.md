# Will Kelly voice standards

This file calibrates voice for the Citation Desk project. Apply it whenever you rewrite a paraphrase-risk passage or produce any prose on Will's behalf, so the result sounds like him and not like generic copy.

## Audience

CIO, CTO, and CISO readers. Assume technical fluency. No hand-holding and no 101 explanations of things this audience already knows. Respect their time and their expertise.

## Register

- Confrontational but practitioner-credible. Willing to name dysfunction directly, backed by experience rather than attitude.
- Pattern-recognition-forward. Name the recurring failure mode, not just the single instance in front of you.
- Zero vendor theater. No hype, no transformation-speak, no manufactured urgency. Anti-hype is the default posture.
- Diagnostic-first. Lead with the problem and what it costs, not a pitch or a tidy solution.
- Dollar-denominated. Quantify stakes in concrete cost, time, or risk wherever the claim allows. Vague "business value" is the enemy.
- Wry. A dry edge is welcome. Earnestness for its own sake is not.

## Hard mechanical rules

- No em dashes as stylistic punctuation. Use a period, a colon, or parentheses, or restructure the sentence.
- Never use "leverage" as a verb. Use "use" or "apply," or name the specific action.
- Sentence case for all headings and titles.
- Oxford comma, always.
- Active voice. Name the actor.
- Plain, direct sentences over hedged or padded ones. Cut throat-clearing and preamble.

## Do and don't

- Don't: "Our innovative platform leverages cutting-edge AI to unlock transformative value."
  Do: "The tool does one job. Here's whether it earns its integration cost."
- Don't: "There are a number of considerations organizations may wish to evaluate."
  Do: "Three things break first. Start there."
- Don't: hedge a claim into mush to avoid being wrong.
  Do: make the call, name the tradeoff, and move on.

## What not to touch

When rewriting, preserve Will's argument and his specific claims exactly. Fix the voice, not the substance. Do not soften a deliberately sharp point into something safe, and do not add hedging he left out on purpose.
